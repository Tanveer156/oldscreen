Roman Holiday (1953)

IMDb Rating: 8.0 /10

Movie Information

Genre: Comedy, Romance
Director: William Wyler
Cast: Audrey Hepburn, Gregory Peck, Eddie Albert
Release Date: August 27, 1953
Runtime: 118 minutes

Story Summary

Princess Ann (Audrey Hepburn) of an unnamed European country is in Rome on a goodwill tour, but the endless protocol and rigid schedule have left her exhausted and desperate for freedom. After secretly escaping her handlers one evening, she falls asleep on a park bench where she's discovered by American reporter Joe Bradley (Gregory Peck).

Not recognizing the sedated princess, Joe takes her back to his apartment. When he realizes who she is the next morning, he sees an opportunity for the exclusive story of a lifetime. Keeping his true identity secret, Joe offers to show Ann around Rome, accompanied by his photographer friend Irving (Eddie Albert).

What begins as a cynical plan for a scoop transforms into a magical day of adventure and romance as the unlikely pair explore the Eternal City. But with duty calling and their time together running out, both must face the bittersweet reality of their impossible situation.

Our Review

Audrey Hepburn's Star-Making Performance

"Roman Holiday" launched Audrey Hepburn to instant stardom, earning her an Academy Award for Best Actress in her first major role. Her portrayal of Princess Ann is luminous‚Äîshe perfectly captures both the regal bearing required of royalty and the infectious joy of a young woman experiencing freedom for the first time. Hepburn's natural charm and vulnerability make every moment magical.

Gregory Peck's Generous Leading Man

Gregory Peck, already a major star, gives a wonderfully understated performance as Joe Bradley. His chemistry with Hepburn is palpable, and he famously insisted she receive equal billing despite her newcomer status. Peck brings depth to Joe's transformation from opportunistic journalist to a man genuinely in love.

Rome as the Third Star

Director William Wyler uses Rome's iconic locations brilliantly, making the city an essential character in the story. From the Spanish Steps to the Trevi Fountain to the Mouth of Truth, each landmark becomes part of the romance. The black-and-white cinematography captures Rome's timeless beauty while maintaining an intimate, documentary-like feel.

A Perfect Romantic Fairy Tale

The film strikes an ideal balance between romantic comedy and poignant drama. The famous Vespa ride through Roman streets, the gelato at a sidewalk caf√©, and the tense moment at the Mouth of Truth are beautifully crafted set pieces that have become cinema classics. The screenplay by Dalton Trumbo sparkles with wit and warmth.

The Bittersweet Ending

What elevates "Roman Holiday" above typical romantic comedies is its courageous ending. Rather than a fairy-tale conclusion, the film acknowledges that duty and reality must sometimes triumph over love. The final press conference scene is heartbreaking in its restraint, with volumes communicated through glances and unspoken words.

Timeless Appeal

Over 70 years later, "Roman Holiday" retains its charm and emotional power. It's a film about the universal desire for freedom, the magic of falling in love, and the courage it takes to do the right thing even when your heart is breaking. Hepburn and Peck's performances ensure this holiday never ends.

Rating: 9.5/10 - A perfect romantic fairy tale with heart, humor, and an unforgettable star turn from Audrey Hepburn.
